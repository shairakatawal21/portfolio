Game Theory / Strategy

Spirit Airlines — Bailout Collapse & Game Theory

Deck ↗Live App ↗
01

The Collapse

May 2, 2026, 3AM: Spirit Airlines ceases all operations. A $500M government bailout collapses at the last moment. Three Wall Street creditors kill it. 17,000 jobs lost, 1.8M passengers stranded. The question: why did no rational actor prevent this outcome?

02

The Game Structure

4 players: U.S. Government (90% equity demanded), Creditor A, Creditor B, Creditor C. Sequential + simultaneous structure. Government moves first (offer). Creditors then play simultaneously: cooperate (accept restructuring) or defect (force liquidation). Payoffs: cooperate alone = worst outcome (diluted, others free ride). Defect = liquidation proceeds, predictable recovery.

Creditor StrategyOthers CooperateOthers Defect
Cooperate6 (best collective)3 (worst individual)
Defect5 (free ride)5 (liquidation floor)
03

Nash Equilibrium & SPNE

Backward induction (SPNE) shows no creditor rationally cooperates without certainty others will follow — dominant strategy is to defect regardless. Nash Equilibrium: all defect → liquidation. This is a textbook coordination failure: the collectively optimal outcome is blocked because no single player can credibly commit to cooperate first. Built as an interactive payoff matrix and game tree deployed as a live Vercel web app.

NIA Impact Capital — Passive vs. Activist Investing

Paper ↗
01

The Question

When does investor passivity enable corporate misconduct? Using the Tesla / Nia Impact Capital dispute over mandatory arbitration to examine the role different investor types play in corporate accountability — and the financial implications of information asymmetry.

02

Framework: Three Investor Types

Passive ("Absentee Landlord")

Index funds and ETFs — low engagement, low cost. By owning everything, passive investors unknowingly subsidize companies hiding legal risk through selective disclosure.

Active (Beat the Market)

Focus on earnings, ratios, and price signals. If social risk appears, they sell and move on — no mechanism to improve the company.

Activist (Change the Company)

Buy to fix. Kristin Hull of Nia Impact Capital challenged Tesla's mandatory arbitration clauses — forcing disclosure of hidden legal liability and improving market efficiency.

03

Argument

Market efficiency requires complete information. When companies classify investments to manipulate reported gains, hide legal problems, or obscure operational losses, investors operate on incomplete information. Shareholder activism — when properly targeted — corrects these negative externalities and restores price discovery.

Finance & Society · F514

Boeing — Agency Theory, Short-Termism & Market Failure

Paper ↗
01

The Case

Boeing's 737 Max crisis is not simply an ethics failure — it is a case study in what happens when markets fail to price long-run risks correctly. Boeing modified the existing 737 platform instead of building a new aircraft, outsourced production to meet cost targets, and delayed safety investments that didn't appear in near-term earnings. Under Friedman's framework, this was rational management responding to correct market incentives.

02

Three Frameworks

Friedman

Firms exist to maximize shareholder profit within the law. Boeing's managers responded to competitive pressure from Airbus. Rational under the framework — even if not defensible in hindsight.

Stiglitz

Markets failed to price safety risk, governance gaps, and liability exposure. The externalized cost (crashes, brand destruction, $20B+ in losses) was never captured in the stock price until it was too late.

Edmans

Long-termism requires pricing all assets — including reputational capital, safety culture, and regulatory relationships. Short-term EPS optimization destroyed far more value than it preserved.

03

Argument

Boeing demonstrates that shareholder value maximization only works when all risks — including long-horizon reputational, regulatory, and safety risks — are correctly priced. The failure was not that Boeing pursued profit; it was that markets rewarded efficient cost management while systematically under-pricing tail risk.

Finance & Society · F514

Gender Pay Gap — Structural Drivers & Capital Market Implications

Paper ↗
01

Beyond the Simple Narrative

Women earn $0.83 per dollar in the U.S. ($0.63 for women of color). Framing this as pure discrimination misses the structural drivers — and ignores the financial market implications of systematically misallocating human capital. As a woman pursuing a finance career, this analysis is not theoretical: it is structural, institutional, and urgent.

02

Four Structural Factors

Occupational Sorting

Men dominate finance, engineering, and technology roles with high wage ceilings and performance-based pay. Statistical clustering, not individual discrimination.

Non-Linear Returns to Hours

High-compensation careers penalize structured schedules disproportionately. Every hour of flexibility has a non-linear cost in career trajectory.

Institutional Promotion

Men are promoted on potential; women on demonstrated proof. This asymmetry compounds over time into large differential earnings.

Motherhood Penalty

Caregiving interruptions break compound career growth at precisely the stage when early investments in relationships and expertise pay off.

03

Capital Market Implication

Labor markets that systematically undervalue talent cannot be fully efficient. The gap is largest at income levels where compensation includes negotiated contracts, equity participation, and discretionary bonuses — precisely where finance careers operate. This is both a social injustice and a market inefficiency.

Finance & Society · F514

The Labor Market — America's Most Important Financial Institution

Op-Ed ↗
01

The Thesis

The labor market is not a statistical indicator — it is America's primary financial institution. Employment quality (wage adequacy relative to cost of living, job stability, benefit access) determines household liquidity, creditworthiness, and lifetime wealth accumulation. Every metric the Fed watches is downstream of this institution's health.

02

Two Simultaneous Crises

Crisis 1: Unemployment ≠ Stability

Historically low unemployment rates mask a growing divergence between job quantity and job quality. Wage growth has not kept pace with cost of living for the bottom 60% of earners. Employment numbers are misleading when the jobs don't provide financial stability.

Crisis 2: AI Decoupling

Artificial Intelligence is automating middle-skill work while increasing returns on high-skill labor — decoupling income from wages and potentially creating a paradigm in which labor is no longer a financial asset but a commodity with ever-changing value.

03

Policy Argument

Policymakers must stop measuring labor quantity and start measuring labor quality: wage adequacy relative to local cost of living, benefit portability (health, retirement) across jobs, and structural resilience to automation. The labor market is infrastructure — and like all infrastructure, it requires intentional investment to function.

Finance & Society · F514

Social Media Firms — Externalities & Shareholder Maximization

Paper ↗
01

The Question

Meta and TikTok generate record valuations while internalizing none of the negative externalities they produce. Is this a failure of markets, regulation, or corporate governance — and what does it mean for how we price these businesses?

02

Three Negative Externalities

Mental Health

50% of U.S. teenagers self-report feeling addicted to social media (Pew, 2025). 25% of teen girls report direct mental health impacts. Algorithmic design maximizes engagement at the cost of wellbeing.

Privacy & Data

Free access is monetized through behavioral data sold to advertisers and third parties. Users face identity theft risk, financial fraud exposure, and targeted manipulation — none of which appear in platform revenue models.

Misinformation

Algorithmic amplification of engagement-maximizing content produces unrealistic lifestyle expectations, social distrust, and measurable political polarization — externalized costs absorbed by society.

03

The Shareholder Value Tension

Under Friedman, social media firms are operating within law and maximizing shareholder value. Under Stiglitz, regulatory failure to price externalities creates a subsidy for harmful business models. The question is whether disclosure requirements, algorithmic accountability, or data liability rules change the calculus — and whether investors should price regulatory risk into these valuations today.
